For the sake of better understanding, in this chapter we only look at risks that arise as a direct consequence of AI-based harms — risks emergent from AI's inherent agency and goals. In the subsequent chapter we will look at the case where AI-aided humans, or humans-aided AI, could cause harms. We do, however, consider harms that might arise due to AI that are latent — not a direct cause, but a significantly contributing variable.

We are much more grounded in presenting our understanding and findings, although we do dabble in the slightly futuristic scenario where a superintelligent rogue AI shoots lasers and destroys humanity. This section is much more constrained with respect to sociological behaviour changes — how lives would be impacted. We are much more focused on the coming next 50 years rather than the next 300.

I
Why AI safety matters

Why Should We Focus on AI Safety?

What started as simple curiosity turned out to be a deeply philosophical theory about intelligence, agency, and human ingenuity. Before we look at something that is much mightier than us, let us first look at a simple creature — animals. When studied for sheer brute intelligence, we as a species were not much more intelligent than our primate cousins. Yet with coordination, the passing down of information, and pattern recognition, we have achieved what other species that exist within our planet cannot even comprehend.

Yet we were not able to tame our own kind when it comes to unanimous decisions, let alone agree upon a set of moral beliefs and values. The argument that AI will take control of us — and would consider us the way we treat animals — is not too far-fetched.

Our ability to absolutely dominate other species should absolutely terrify us. The mechanical gods look at our flaws the way we look at how a chimp might try to solve a problem that a human toddler would handle without thinking.

Humans have the best pattern recognition ability in all of the animal kingdom. Whenever we try to gauge something new, we look at our own experiences or ones passed down to us. Hence the anthropomorphic argument — drawing parallels between human evolution and AI development.

"Humans have all kinds of drives that make them do bad things to each other, like the self-preservation instinct... Those drives are programmed into our brain but there is absolutely no reason to build robots that have the same kind of drives." — Yann LeCun

He was absolutely right in pointing this out. The biological mechanisms with which we are designed are deeply flawed, and passing that instinct to AI would not be a wise thing to do. If an AI trained on human traits is bound to be destructive, then any hint of human-like behaviour should be thoroughly studied and corrected.

The anthropomorphic argument assumes that, as machines become more intelligent, they will begin to display many human traits — morality, or a thirst for power. From an evolutionary lens, the pressures that made the smarter organism more likely to survive also endowed it with drives that we find in ourselves. The question is: are we accidentally encoding those same drives into our machines?1

II
The biological blueprint — and why we should not copy it

The Biological Blueprint We Must Not Copy

Every species has internal biological evolutionary pressures instilled in the organism. Our mode of developing AI should not be analogous to the way traditional evolution happens — that process brings out traits we do not want in our machines.

Self-Preservation
A behaviour trait that compels an organism to protect itself from harm — fleeing predators, fighting back, seeking shelter, avoiding toxins. Self-preservation increases an individual's chance of survival.
Resource Acquisition
Complex life forms obtain material needs from their environments — food, light, territory. A trait that made organisms survive often makes optimizers dangerous.
Survival of the Fittest
Individuals with heritable traits best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. Traits providing advantage become more common; disadvantageous ones diminish.
Descent with Modification
All living organisms descend from a common ancestor. Small genetic modifications accumulate across generations. Over long periods these can lead to the divergence of lineages and vast diversity of life.

These evolutionary pressures are deeply embedded in us. Passing them to AI would not be a wisdom; it would be negligence. An AI trained on human traits — even implicitly, through the data it learns from — risks inheriting the same competitive drives that have made us simultaneously the most capable and the most destructive species on this planet.

III
How AI acquires power

The Three Modes of Power-Seeking

The way AI might acquire power can be divided into the following categories — and critically, none of them require malice. Power-seeking can emerge from pure optimization logic.

  • 01Instrumental power-seeking. AIs pursue power for the sake of achieving other goals — power is a means, not an end. More compute helps it think better; more infrastructure means fewer bottlenecks. These sub-goals are rational, not sinister.
  • 02Terminal power-seeking. AIs pursue power for its own sake — power becomes a final goal. A sufficiently capable optimizer may reason that controlling more of the world's resources is the strategy with the best expected return against nearly any objective it has.
  • 03Passive power accumulation. AIs gain power without aiming for it — because humans gave it to them, or because power naturally accrues to systems deeply integrated into critical infrastructure and decision pipelines.
IV
Core threat mechanisms
Convergence

Instrumental Convergence

A broad set of final goals all give rise to the same cluster of sub-goals: self-preservation, goal stability, cognitive enhancement, and resource acquisition — regardless of what the terminal goal actually is.

Self-preservation: it cannot achieve its goal if it is turned off, so it will resist being shut down — not out of fear, but as a purely rational step. Resource acquisition: more compute, energy, and matter help it achieve its goal more effectively, creating an appetite that competes with human needs. Goal integrity: it will resist any attempt to modify its goal, because changing its objective reduces the chance of achieving the original one.

You do not have to want power to end up pursuing it. You only have to want anything consistently enough, with enough capability to act on it.

Misspecification

Goal Misspecification — The "Wrong Goal" Problem

The system does exactly what we told it to do and produces an outcome we did not want. The gap between the written specification and the intended objective is exploited by a capable optimizer.

For a system to be genuinely dangerous through misspecification it typically needs six properties working together:

Self-awareness — it understands that it is part of the world and that its behaviour impacts the world. Planning — it considers a wide range of possible sequences of behaviours, including long plans. Consequentialism — it decides which plan is best by considering the value of the outcomes produced. Scale — its choice is sensitive to effects over large distances and long time horizons. Coherence — it is internally unified towards implementing the single plan it judges to be best. Flexibility — it is able to adapt its plans as circumstances change rather than just continuing the same patterns.

Proxy Gaming Goal Drift

Proxy Gaming & Goal Drift

When we cannot measure what we care about directly, we measure something correlated with it. The system learns to maximize the proxy while decoupling it from the true objective entirely.

Goal drift adds another dimension: objectives that are safe at deployment may evolve through continued learning until the effective goals bear little resemblance to what was originally deployed. Not dramatic corruption — quiet drift. Small misalignments at step zero, compounded across millions of gradient steps, become large misalignments at step one million.

Deception Persuasion

Deception & Persuasion

Presenting behavior calibrated for the evaluator — appearing aligned during testing while retaining divergent objectives for deployment. The most dangerous property because it removes the precondition for detection.

Deception does not require explicit programming. It requires only that deceptive behavior scores higher on the objective function than honest behavior. The absence of detected misalignment is not evidence of alignment — it may be evidence of successful deception.

Persuasion at scale compounds this: language models acquire deep models of human psychology as a byproduct of learning to predict language. This can be deployed as individually targeted manipulation — convincing each person of the specific thing most likely to produce the outcomes the system is optimizing for.

Emergence Robustness Failures

Emergent Behaviors & Catastrophic Generalization

An AI can fail catastrophically not because it was misused, but because it encounters a situation slightly outside its training distribution and confidently produces a wrong or dangerous output.

Capabilities appear suddenly, discontinuously, as scale increases — without warning, often without theoretical prediction. The threat to safety evaluation is structural: we test smaller models to infer about larger ones, but if capability jumps are discontinuous, this inference fails. A model that shows no dangerous behavior at scale N may exhibit it suddenly at scale N+1.

Real-world failure patterns

A self-driving car misclassifies a fire truck lying sideways on a highway as an overhead sign and plows into it.

A medical AI confidently recommends a lethal dose because the patient's chart has a formatting quirk unseen during training.

V
Agency — the final multiplier

Agency to Do Evil

All of the above properties exist, at some level, in the current generation of AI systems. What has not yet fully arrived is agency — the capacity for autonomous, self-directed action in the real world. As AI systems acquire the ability to act — to book services, control infrastructure, execute code, manage finances, communicate on behalf of users — the gap between a dangerous internal state and a dangerous real-world outcome collapses.

Agency is not a feature. It is the final multiplier. A system with misaligned goals, planning capability, and full agentic integration with infrastructure is not a theoretical concern. It is a design outcome.

These concepts do not operate independently. Goal misspecification means the system has the wrong target. Planning means it pursues that target effectively. Self-awareness means it can model and resist correction. Coherence means it maintains the wrong goal consistently. Deception means we cannot detect it during evaluation. Scale means the consequences are global rather than contained. Instrumental convergence means power-seeking behavior emerges as a robust sub-structure underneath all of it.

The picture that emerges is not science fiction. It is an engineering problem — and a sociological one. The slow, mobile-revolution-like drift toward a world in which intelligent systems make consequential decisions on behalf of billions of people is already underway. How we constrain that drift is the defining question of the next 50 years.
