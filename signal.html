// 01 — INTRODUCTION

THE INTELLIGENCE
ARMS RACE

The battlefield is no longer defined by soldiers, tanks, or missiles. It is defined by data pipelines, inference latency, and the geopolitics of silicon.

For the first time in modern history, the most consequential military technology is being developed not in government labs but in corporate headquarters. The implications for global power, international law, and human survival are profound — and deeply underexplored.

Autonomous systems are already making targeting decisions with human oversight measured in milliseconds. AI-driven logistics are reshaping supply chains in active conflict zones. And the policy frameworks designed to govern these tools are a generation behind the technology.

// 02 — THE SILICON CURTAIN

CHIPS, CHOKEPOINTS
& COLD LOGIC

Control the chip, control the model. Control the model, control the war.

The US CHIPS Act and its successive export restrictions on advanced semiconductors represent perhaps the most consequential act of economic statecraft in decades. By denying access to NVIDIA's H100 and A100 architectures, Washington has erected what analysts now call the Silicon Curtain — a technological boundary as significant as the Iron Curtain was ideologically.

// POLICY BRIEF
THE WASSENAAR PROBLEM
The Wassenaar Arrangement was never designed for software. It categorizes by hardware: missiles, lasers, sensors. But the most dangerous military AI is a 70 billion parameter model running on commercially available cloud infrastructure. It has no weight class, no serial number, no point of interdiction. The regime is structurally blind to the actual threat.

// 04 — THE GOVERNANCE GAP

WHO GOVERNS
THE MACHINE?

International humanitarian law was written for humans. The Geneva Conventions assume a soldier who can distinguish combatant from civilian. Autonomous systems cannot be court-martialed.

The fundamental problem of AI weapons governance is attribution. When an autonomous system commits a war crime, accountability dissolves across developers, deployers, commanders, and algorithms. No existing legal framework can allocate responsibility across that chain. The result is not impunity — it is structural impunity, baked into the architecture.

// THREE GOVERNANCE MODELS
PROHIBIT · REGULATE · SOVEREIGNTY
Three frameworks compete: the Prohibitionist approach (ICRC, Austria) seeks an outright ban on lethal autonomous systems. The Regulatory approach (EU, Canada) seeks binding transparency standards. The Sovereignty approach (US, China, Russia, Israel) treats AI weapons doctrine as beyond international scrutiny. None has achieved consensus. All are already obsolete.
// SIGNAL EDITORIAL
THE SHAPE OF WHAT COMES NEXT
The machine has already changed the shape of war. The question before us is not whether to allow it, but whether civilization can develop the institutional capacity to govern what it has already unleashed. History does not offer optimism — but it offers the example of the nuclear age: imperfect, costly, and occasionally terrifying. Yet somehow, so far, sufficient.
