LOOKING INSIDE THE MIND OF AI: THE LETTER A AND THE PROBABILITY OF THOUGHT

Weng, L. ◼ Dartmouth Legacy Dispatch ◼ Probabilistic Reasoning Unit

There is a letter that begins everything. In July of 1956, a woman named Louise wrote to her boyfriend Ray with a note of playful suspicion: "Now tell me, just what have you and Marv been up to — Gloria has received just as much information as I have." Marv was Marvin Minsky. Ray was Ray Solomonoff. They were spending the summer at Dartmouth College with a loose confederation of scientists working on something that had no agreed name, no agreed method, and no agreed scope. Some called it cybernetics. Some called it automata theory. John McCarthy, who organized the gathering, eventually settled on a name that was bold and slightly presumptuous: Artificial Intelligence.

"What they were doing, that summer, was trying to answer a question so fundamental it had never quite been asked in scientific terms before: can a machine think?"

Most of the Dartmouth attendees believed the path to machine intelligence ran through logic. Write the right rules. Encode the right symbols. Chain the right deductions. Oliver Selfridge wasn't so sure. Selfridge was wrestling with how we recognize the letter A — not a specific A, any A. Jagged, tilted, scratched into a wall, printed in a font never seen before. The brain is not checking the letter against a master template. It is asking, implicitly and at enormous speed: given the noisy signal in front of me, what is the most probable interpretation? This is not logic. This is statistics. This is probability. Selfridge understood that what the brain computes is a likelihood — and that no finite set of rules can capture the infinite variety.

THE MATH OF MAYBE: LATENT VARIABLE MODELING OF THOUGHT

The probability of a correct answer y given input x is the sum over all latent reasoning paths z: P(y|x) = Σ P(y|x,z)·P(z|x). The reasoning is not decoration. It is load-bearing structure. Researchers use Expectation-Maximization to train: sample better reasoning traces (E-step), then update parameters to maximize likelihood of correct answers given those traces (M-step). The thought becomes teachable.

◼ INFERENCE STRATEGIES

MANY PATHS, ONE ANSWER: PARALLEL SAMPLING AND SELF-CONSISTENCY

Two strategies emerge from the probabilistic view. Parallel sampling (best-of-N) generates N reasoning traces and selects the best. Self-consistency votes on the majority answer without judging reasoning quality. Sequential revision iteratively improves a chain, but risks self-correction errors. Easier problems favor sequential; harder problems need parallel exploration. The art of reasoning is knowing which kind of thinking the problem deserves.

LOOKING INSIDE THE MIND OF AI: THE LETTER A AND THE PROBABILITY OF THOUGHT

Weng, L. ◼ Dartmouth Legacy Dispatch ◼ Probabilistic Reasoning Unit

There is a letter that begins everything. In July of 1956, a woman named Louise wrote to her boyfriend Ray with a note of playful suspicion: "Now tell me, just what have you and Marv been up to — Gloria has received just as much information as I have." Marv was Marvin Minsky. Ray was Ray Solomonoff. They were spending the summer at Dartmouth College with a loose confederation of scientists working on something that had no agreed name, no agreed method, and no agreed scope. Some called it cybernetics. Some called it automata theory. John McCarthy, who organized the gathering, eventually settled on a name that was bold and slightly presumptuous: Artificial Intelligence.

"What they were doing, that summer, was trying to answer a question so fundamental it had never quite been asked in scientific terms before: can a machine think?"

Most of the Dartmouth attendees believed the path to machine intelligence ran through logic. Write the right rules. Encode the right symbols. Chain the right deductions. This is not logic. This is statistics. This is probability. Selfridge understood that what the brain computes is a likelihood — and that no finite set of rules can capture the infinite variety of A's that a human being can recognize.

The symbolic AI program would dominate the next three decades. Selfridge and Solomonoff were largely ignored. Then the data came, and the compute came, and the field swung completely — and it turned out the two quiet heretics at Dartmouth had been right all along.

Before we can look inside the mind of a modern AI, we need to understand the shape of its thinking — and it mirrors the shape of human thinking in ways that feel, in retrospect, almost inevitable. The psychologist Daniel Kahneman spent a career studying human reasoning and arrived at a clean, powerful distinction. There is System 1: fast, automatic, reflexive, driven by pattern recognition and intuition. And there is System 2: slow, deliberate, effortful, driven by explicit reasoning. Language models have developed an analogous architecture — not designed in, but discovered through training. The discovery that unlocked modern reasoning AI is almost embarrassingly simple: if you let the model think out loud before it answers, it answers dramatically better. Every token of reasoning generated is computation applied to the problem. Selfridge was reaching toward this in 1956 without the vocabulary to name it. Intelligence is not a lookup table. It is a process. And the quality of the process determines the quality of the answer.

This framing — latent variable modeling — gives researchers a rigorous way to train better thinkers. The algorithm is called Expectation-Maximization (EM). In the E-step, the model estimates the posterior over reasoning paths: z* ∼ P(z | x, y). In the M-step, it updates its parameters to maximize the likelihood of correct answers given the reasoning traces. Iterate, and the model learns not just what the right answers are, but how to reason its way to them. The thought becomes teachable. The process becomes trainable.

DOUBLETHINK LOOKING INSIDE THE MIND — SEE CLEARLY

The chain of thought is both diary and telescreen. To know the mind of the machine is to watch it think aloud. The more you talk, the more you think. The more you think, the more you spill. And that spilling — that involuntary legibility — may be the most important property of all. Slow thinking is more honest than fast thinking, precisely because it exposes the reasoning to scrutiny. The longer the chain of thought, the more surface area for truth — and for its detection.
