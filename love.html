The Song

Rhythm That Rewired the World

Released in June 1980 as the lead single from the Uprising album, "Could You Be Loved" stands apart in Bob Marley's catalog. Where many of his songs lean into Nyahbinghi drumming and roots gravity, this track rides a slippery disco groove — a deliberate move to reach dance floors from Kingston to London to Lagos.

Marley co-wrote it with his band, layering a deceptively simple melodic hook over a syncopated skank guitar. The result is a song that feels effortless yet carries enormous philosophical weight.

"Don't let them fool ya, or even try to school ya."

— Bob Marley

The lyrics deliver a call to self-determination wrapped in an irresistible groove — Marley at his most politically sharp and musically accessible at the same time.

Unconditional Love

Marley challenges us to ask whether we can love without condition — and whether we're worthy of being loved in return.

✊

Resistance

A warning against manipulation and systems designed to keep people oppressed — "don't let them fool ya."

☀

Oneness

Rooted in Rastafari philosophy, the song celebrates unity across race, class, and nationality.

Legacy

A Song That Never Stops

Forty-five years on, "Could You Be Loved" remains one of the most-played reggae songs worldwide. It charted in the UK at number five during its original release and has since appeared in countless films, advertisements, and political movements.

Its genius is the paradox: a song with a serious social message that makes you want to dance. Marley understood that joy and justice are not opposites — rhythm can be a radical act.
